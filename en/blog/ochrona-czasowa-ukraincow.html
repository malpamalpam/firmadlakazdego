Temporary Protection for Ukrainians After March 4, 2026 — What's Changing?

Grzegorz Stepien · 24.02.2026

Transition from the emergency system to the general one

March 4, 2026 marks a turning point for Ukrainian citizens residing in Poland under temporary protection. The Ukrainian special act, which since March 2022 has been the legal basis for the stay and work of hundreds of thousands of refugees, is being replaced by general law mechanisms. The European Union has extended temporary protection until March 2027, but Poland is introducing its own transitional solutions.

This change means that the previous simplified procedures are being gradually phased out. In their place come standard residence legalization paths — temporary residence permits, permanent residence permits, or EU long-term resident permits. People who have so far stayed in Poland solely on the basis of the special act must take concrete steps to regulate their status.

A key element is a 6-month transitional period during which previous entitlements remain valid. This gives time to submit appropriate residence applications and gather the required documentation. However, experts recommend not waiting until the last moment — voivodeship offices may be burdened with a large number of applications.

PESEL UKR — registration within 30 days

One of the most important changes is the requirement to register the PESEL UKR number within 30 days of the new regulations coming into force. PESEL UKR is a special identification number assigned to Ukrainian citizens who arrived in Poland after February 24, 2022. Previously, registration was possible but not mandatory within such a strict deadline.

Registration can be done in person at any commune office throughout Poland. A valid identity document (Ukrainian passport or another document confirming identity), a photo, and a completed form are needed for registration. The process is free of charge and usually takes about fifteen minutes.

The PESEL UKR number is essential for using public services, the healthcare system, submitting residence applications, and signing employment contracts. Failure to register within the required deadline may result in loss of access to social benefits and difficulties in legalizing one's stay.

Digital documentation (DIIA and mObywatel)

Another significant change is the transition to digital documentation. The Ukrainian DIIA application is officially recognized as a source of identity documents in Poland. Digital DIIA cards replace paper documents, which significantly simplifies formalities and speeds up service at offices.

At the same time, Ukrainian citizens with a PESEL UKR number gain access to the Polish mObywatel application, where they can store their documents in digital form. The integration of both systems enables identity verification without the need to carry paper documents.

It is worth noting that not all institutions and private entities are yet prepared to handle digital documents. When visiting offices, banks, or signing important contracts, it is recommended to also carry a traditional identity document as a backup.

Right to work

Ukrainian citizens covered by temporary protection retain the right to work in Poland without the need to obtain a work permit. This is one of the key entitlements that remains unchanged after March 4, 2026. Employers only need to report the employment to the relevant labor office within 14 days of the foreigner starting work.

The condition for maintaining the right to work is having a valid PESEL UKR number and legal residence on Polish territory. People who fail to register their PESEL UKR or do not submit a residence application during the transitional period may lose their right to legal employment.

For employers, the change means the necessity to verify the residence status of employed Ukrainian citizens. It is recommended to update HR documentation and ensure that all employees have a current PESEL UKR. Penalties for illegal employment of a foreigner can reach up to PLN 30,000.

Social benefits

After March 4, 2026, the social benefits system for Ukrainian citizens undergoes significant modification. The previous model of universal access to benefits is being replaced by a system targeted at particularly vulnerable groups — the elderly, families with children, people with disabilities, and people in difficult financial situations.

The 800+ benefit (formerly 500+) remains available to Ukrainian citizens with a registered PESEL UKR who legally reside in Poland. Access to public healthcare also remains unchanged, provided one has a valid residence status.

One-time assistance benefits for newly arrived refugees are being limited. People arriving in Poland after March 4, 2026 are subject to standard asylum procedures and do not automatically qualify for benefits provided under the special act.

Running a business

Ukrainian citizens who established a business in Poland before March 5, 2026 can continue it on the same terms. This applies to both sole proprietorships (JDG) registered in CEIDG and commercial law companies entered in the National Court Register (KRS).

However, new regulations introduce the requirement of having a valid residence title as a condition for running a business. This means that entrepreneurs must ensure their residence status is regulated during the transitional period. Lack of a valid residence permit may result in the need to suspend or close the business.

For those who are just planning to start a business in Poland, an alternative may be to use a business incubator. An incubator allows you to run a business without the need to register your own company, which eliminates some of the formalities related to residence status. The Firma Dla Każdego Foundation offers support in this area in both Polish and Ukrainian.
