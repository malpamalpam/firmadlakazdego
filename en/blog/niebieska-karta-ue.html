EU Blue Card After June 1, 2025 — New Mobility Rules

What is the EU Blue Card?

The EU Blue Card is a residence and work permit intended for highly qualified workers from outside the European Union. It was introduced by an EU directive to attract talent to member states and is the European equivalent of the American Green Card, though with different awarding rules.

In Poland, the Blue Card is issued as a temporary residence permit for the purpose of performing work requiring high qualifications. The card holder receives a residence card with the annotation "EU Blue Card," which entitles them to legal residence and work in Poland and — after meeting conditions — to mobility within the entire European Union.

Until now, the Blue Card has not been very popular in Poland due to restrictive conditions and limited mobility. The reform that came into force on June 1, 2025, aims to change this, making the instrument more attractive for both employees and employers.

Key changes from June 1, 2025

The most important change is the expansion of the definition of "high qualifications." Previously, a diploma from higher education lasting at least 3 years was required. The new regulations also allow people with at least 3 years of documented professional experience in a position requiring comparable skills, even without formal higher education.

The salary threshold required to obtain a Blue Card has also been lowered. Previously, the salary had to be at least 150% of the average wage in the country. The new threshold is 100-140% of the average wage (depending on the sector), which in Poland in 2026 means approximately PLN 8,000 to PLN 11,200 gross per month.

Facilitations have also been introduced for people with international protection beneficiary status — they can apply for a Blue Card without losing their existing protection status. This is an important change that opens a career path for refugees with high qualifications.

Who can apply for the Blue Card?

A third-country national (from outside the EU/EEA/Switzerland) who simultaneously meets the following conditions may apply for the EU Blue Card: possesses higher professional qualifications (higher education diploma or at least 3 years of professional experience in the field), has an employment contract or binding job offer for at least 6 months, and earns a salary at the required level.

The new regulations expand the range of qualifying professions. In addition to traditional sectors such as IT, engineering, medicine, and finance, the Blue Card is now also available for specialists in artificial intelligence, cybersecurity, green technologies, and project management. The list of deficit sectors with lower salary thresholds is updated annually.

It is worth noting that the Blue Card is not available for self-employed persons — it is an instrument intended exclusively for hired employees. Foreigners planning to run a business in Poland should consider other forms of residence legalization, such as a residence permit for the purpose of conducting business activity.

Application procedure

The EU Blue Card application is submitted to the voivode competent for the foreigner's place of residence (or intended place of residence for people staying abroad). From 2026, the application can be submitted electronically through the MOS system or in person at the voivodeship office.

The application should be accompanied by: a completed application form, a current biometric photo, a copy of the passport, documents confirming qualifications (diploma or certificate of professional experience), an employment contract or job offer with a specified salary, health insurance, and confirmation of having a place of residence.

The application processing time is up to 90 days from the date of submitting a complete application. In practice, thanks to the MOS system, complete applications without reservations are processed within 30-60 days. The Blue Card is issued for the period of the employment contract plus 3 months, but not longer than 4 years.

New EU mobility rules

The biggest revolution in the Blue Card reform are the new intra-EU mobility rules. Previously, a Blue Card holder could move to another EU country only after 18 months of legal residence in the first member state. The new regulations shorten this period to 12 months.

Moreover, the possibility of short-term business mobility without the need to obtain additional permits has been introduced. A Blue Card holder issued in Poland can stay and work in another EU state for up to 90 days within 180 days for business purposes — client meetings, training, industry conferences.

For long-term mobility (over 90 days), it is still necessary to submit a Blue Card application in the destination country, but the procedure is significantly simplified. The new country must process the application within 30 days, and the card holder can start working from the day of application submission, without waiting for the decision.

Benefits for employees and employers

For employees, the key benefit is the path to permanent residence — after 5 years of legal residence on the Blue Card (of which at least 2 years in the last country), the holder can apply for EU long-term resident status with the annotation "former EU Blue Card holder," which gives additional mobility rights.

The new regulations also allow easier family reunification. Family members of the Blue Card holder (spouse and minor children) can apply for a residence permit in a simplified procedure, with the right to work without additional permits. The family application can be submitted simultaneously with the main application.

Employers, on the other hand, gain access to a broader pool of qualified candidates from around the world. The recruitment procedure with the Blue Card is simpler than a standard work permit — it does not require a labor market test (checking whether there are candidates from the EU for the position). This significantly speeds up the process of hiring specialists from abroad.
